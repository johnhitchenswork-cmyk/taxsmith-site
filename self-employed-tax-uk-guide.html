Self Employed Tax in the UK: A Plain Guide for Sole Traders

The hardest part of being self employed is rarely the work. It is the quiet worry that you do not really know how much tax you owe, and the fear that the self assessment bill will be bigger than the money you have left. This guide explains, in plain terms, how tax works when you work for yourself in the UK, so you can plan with a clear head rather than a knot in your stomach.

A practical, UK focused overview written for sole traders and freelancers, not accountants.

Why self employed tax feels confusing

When you are employed, tax and National Insurance are taken off your pay before you ever see it. You get a payslip, the numbers are sorted, and you rarely have to think about it. When you are self employed, none of that happens. The full amount your clients pay lands in your account, and it is on you to work out how much of it is really yours to keep and how much belongs to the tax man later.

That gap, between the money arriving and the bill arriving, is where the stress lives. The fix is to understand the moving parts and to estimate them early.

The two main things you pay

As a self employed sole trader in the UK, there are two main things to plan for on your profit:

  • Income tax on your profit, which is your income minus your allowable business costs.
  • National Insurance for self employed people, which is separate from income tax and easy to forget.

Both are worked out on your profit for the tax year, not on every pound that comes in. That is an important point: you are taxed on what you keep after legitimate business costs, not on your total takings.

Income tax on your profit

Income tax in the UK works in bands. A portion of your income is covered by a personal allowance, and income above that is taxed at rates that step up as you earn more. Because the exact thresholds and rates are set by the government and can change from year to year, the safe approach is to understand the shape of it, lower income taxed at lower rates, higher income at higher rates, and confirm the current figures on the HMRC website rather than relying on a number you half remember.

National Insurance for the self employed

Self employed National Insurance is the part that catches people out, because it does not exist in the same way for the employed. It is charged on your profit once it passes a threshold, and it sits on top of your income tax. When you estimate what to set aside, leaving National Insurance out is one of the most common reasons a bill ends up larger than expected.

What self assessment actually is

Self assessment is simply the system HMRC uses to collect tax from people whose income is not taxed at source. You report your income and costs for the tax year, HMRC works out what is due, and you pay it. The process has deadlines, and missing them can lead to penalties, so it pays to know roughly what you owe well before the date arrives.

The principle: a self employed person who has set money aside through the year reaches self assessment calm. The one who has not is the one scrambling in January.

How to plan for it through the year

The single most useful habit is to set aside a share of every payment as it comes in, into a separate pot, so the tax money is never spent by accident. To do that with confidence, you need a rough idea of what your overall tax and National Insurance will be.

  1. Estimate your profit for the year as best you can.
  2. Estimate the income tax and National Insurance on that profit.
  3. Work out roughly what share of your income that represents.
  4. Move that share of every payment into a separate savings pot.

Do this and the bill stops being a shock. It becomes a number you have already been quietly saving towards.

FAQ

Do I pay tax on everything my clients pay me?

No. You pay tax on your profit, which is your income minus your allowable business costs. Keeping good records of those costs is what stops you paying more tax than you need to. See our guide to allowable expenses for the self employed.

When is the self assessment deadline?

HMRC sets the deadlines for registering, filing and paying, and they fall at fixed points after the tax year ends. Because the exact dates matter and penalties apply for missing them, check the current deadlines on the HMRC website and put them in your calendar early.

How much should I set aside for tax?

It depends on your income, because higher earnings are taxed at higher rates. The reliable way is to estimate your income tax and National Insurance for the year, work out the share of income that represents, and save that share of every payment. A calculator makes this quick.

Do I still need an accountant?

Many self employed people manage their own self assessment, while others prefer an accountant for peace of mind, especially as their income grows. Either way, estimating your tax through the year helps you stay in control and have a sensible conversation if you do get professional help.
