Tax on a Side Hustle in the UK: 7 Things to Know Before You Earn

A side hustle is one of the best ways to add to your income, but the moment money starts coming in, a quiet question follows: do I owe tax on this, and how does it sit alongside my day job? The good news is that side hustle tax is not complicated once you understand a few key points. Here are seven things worth knowing before the income adds up.

A plain UK guide for anyone earning on the side, whether that is selling, freelancing, or a small venture alongside a job.

1. Extra income can be taxable, even with a day job

If your day job already uses your tax allowances, income from a side hustle generally stacks on top and can be taxable in its own right. The fact that tax is taken off your salary automatically does not mean your side income is covered. It usually is not, which is why people get caught out.

2. There is a point where it becomes reportable

HMRC sets a threshold below which small amounts of extra trading income do not need to be reported, and above which you do. Because that figure is set by HMRC and can change, the safe move is to check the current allowance on the HMRC website. The principle to remember: a genuinely tiny side income may sit under the line, but a growing one will cross it, and at that point you need to tell HMRC.

3. Your side hustle profit is what gets taxed

Just like a sole trader, you are taxed on profit, not turnover. The costs of running your side hustle, the materials, the listing fees, the relevant tools, can be allowable expenses that reduce the figure you are taxed on. Keeping a simple record of those costs from day one is worth real money.

The principle: a side hustle is a small business in the eyes of tax. Profit, not takings, is the number that matters.

4. It sits on top of your other income

This is the part that surprises people most. Because your side hustle income is added to your employment income, it can be taxed at the rate that applies once everything is stacked together. So the tax on your side hustle is not always the lowest rate, it depends on what you already earn. This is exactly why estimating it matters before you spend the money.

5. You will likely need self assessment

Once your side income passes the reportable point, you will usually register for self assessment and report it each year. It sounds daunting, but it is simply telling HMRC what you earned and what it cost you. The earlier you register and understand it, the calmer the process is. Keep an eye on the deadlines, as penalties apply for missing them.

6. Set money aside from the start

The single habit that keeps side hustlers out of trouble is moving a share of every payment into a separate pot for tax. Spend everything as it arrives and the bill can feel like a punishment. Set aside a sensible share and the bill is just money you had already put away. To pick the right share, you need an estimate of what you will owe.

7. A quick estimate beats a January shock

You do not need to wait until your return to know roughly where you stand. Estimating your side hustle tax through the year tells you what to save and removes the fear of the unknown. That is the whole point: small, regular clarity instead of one big surprise.

FAQ

Do I have to declare a small side income?

There is a threshold for small amounts of trading income below which you may not need to report, set by HMRC and subject to change. Above it, you do need to report. Check the current allowance on the HMRC website and keep records so you know where you stand.

How is my side hustle taxed if I also have a job?

It is added on top of your employment income, so it can be taxed at the rate that applies to your combined total. That is why a side hustle pound is not always taxed at the lowest rate, and why estimating early helps.

Can I claim expenses on a side hustle?

Yes. The legitimate costs of running it reduce your taxable profit, the same way they do for any sole trader. See our guide to allowable expenses for the self employed.

How much should I put aside?

Enough to cover the income tax and National Insurance on the profit, which depends on your overall income. Estimating it gives you a sensible share to save from each payment. Our plain guide to self employed tax explains the moving parts.
