Payments on account: what the 31 July bill is and who owes it

If you are self employed and a second tax bill lands in the summer, it can feel like HMRC has invented a new charge out of nowhere. It has not. The 31 July payment is a payment on account, an advance towards next year's tax, and once you understand how it works it stops being a shock and becomes a date you can plan for. This guide explains what it is, who has to make one, how it is worked out, and how to reduce it honestly when your profits have genuinely fallen.

A plain UK explanation for sole traders and freelancers, written to help you plan, not to give formal tax advice.

What a payment on account actually is

A payment on account is a payment towards your next Self Assessment bill, made before that bill is even finalised. HMRC assumes your next year of profit will be broadly similar to last year, so it asks you to pay some of the expected tax in advance rather than all in one go the following January. For the self employed, this advance includes your Class 4 National Insurance as well as your income tax.

It is collected in two instalments. The first is due by midnight on 31 January, alongside any balancing payment for the year just gone. The second is due by midnight on 31 July. That July instalment is the one that surprises people, because the work it relates to has not happened yet.

Who has to make one

You are not always asked for payments on account. HMRC does not require them if either of the following applies:

  • Your last Self Assessment bill was under 1,000 pounds.
  • You paid more than 80 percent of the tax you owed at source, for example through a tax code on employment income.

If neither of those lets you off, and your bill was 1,000 pounds or more with most of it not collected at source, you will usually be asked to make payments on account for the following year. These figures are set by HMRC and can change, so if you are close to a threshold it is worth confirming the current position on the HMRC website.

How each instalment is calculated

The maths is deliberately simple. Each payment on account is usually half of the total tax you owed for the previous year. So if last year's bill, income tax and Class 4 National Insurance combined, came to a certain amount, HMRC splits it into two equal halves and asks for one on 31 January and one on 31 July.

The key idea: you are prepaying next year's tax in two halves, based on last year's figure. When you actually file, the real bill is worked out and your two advance payments are credited against it.

A worked example

Suppose a sole trader's total Self Assessment bill for the year, income tax plus Class 4 National Insurance, came to 4,000 pounds and none of it was collected at source. HMRC would typically set two payments on account of 2,000 pounds each: one due 31 January, one due 31 July. When that trader files the next return and the real bill is known, those 4,000 pounds of advance payments are set against it. If the real bill is higher, a balancing payment is due the following January. If it is lower, the difference is repaid or carried forward. The figures used here are illustrative to show the mechanism, not a quoted rate.

How to reduce your payments on account honestly

If you genuinely expect to earn less than the year your payments were based on, perhaps you have taken on fewer clients or wound a project down, you can claim to reduce your payments on account. You do this through your online Self Assessment account or by submitting form SA303. This is a legitimate step, not a loophole.

The important caution is this: if you reduce your payments too far and your actual bill turns out higher, HMRC charges interest on the shortfall from the date the payment was originally due. So reduce based on a genuine, considered estimate of lower profit, not on hope. Under-claiming a reduction is safer than over-claiming one.

What happens if you miss 31 July

If the July instalment is not paid on time, interest starts to accrue on the outstanding amount from the deadline until it is paid. The mechanism is interest on the balance, not an instant fixed fine on this particular payment, but it still adds up and it is entirely avoidable. If you cannot pay in full, the right move is to contact HMRC before the deadline rather than after. A Time to Pay arrangement can spread the cost, and reaching out early is always viewed better than going quiet.

FAQ

Is the 31 July payment a separate tax?

No. It is not an extra tax. It is an advance instalment towards next year's Self Assessment bill, including your Class 4 National Insurance. It is credited against that bill when you file, so you are paying the same tax earlier, not paying more of it.

Do I always have to make payments on account?

No. You are not asked for them if your last bill was under 1,000 pounds, or if more than 80 percent of the tax you owed was collected at source. Otherwise HMRC will usually require them.

Can I reduce my payment on account?

Yes, if you genuinely expect lower profits. You claim through your Self Assessment account or on form SA303. Reduce it too far and HMRC charges interest on the shortfall, so base any reduction on a real estimate.

What if I cannot pay by 31 July?

Interest builds on anything unpaid after the deadline. If you cannot pay in full, contact HMRC before the date. A Time to Pay arrangement can spread the amount, and asking early is far better than saying nothing.
