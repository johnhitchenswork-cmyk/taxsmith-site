Allowable Expenses for the Self Employed: What You Can Claim

Every pound you spend running your business that counts as an allowable expense is a pound that is not taxed as profit. Yet many self employed people leave money on the table simply because they do not record their costs, or they are not sure what they are allowed to claim. This guide explains, in plain UK terms, what an allowable expense is, the common categories to look at, and why good records quietly lower your tax bill.

A practical overview for sole traders and freelancers, written to help you keep more of what you earn, legitimately.

What an allowable expense actually is

An allowable expense is a cost you incur wholly and exclusively for the purposes of your business. Because you are taxed on your profit, which is income minus allowable costs, recording these properly means you are not paying tax on money you had to spend to do the work. The key test is that the cost is genuinely for the business, not personal spending dressed up as a business cost.

The principle: you are taxed on what you keep, not on what you turn over. Allowable expenses are how the system recognises the cost of doing the work.

Common categories self employed people can claim

The exact rules and any limits are set by HMRC and can change, so treat this as a map of where to look rather than a final word. Common allowable expense areas for a sole trader include:

  • Office costs: stationery, printing, postage, and the software or subscriptions you use for the business.
  • Equipment and tools: the things you need to do the work, such as a laptop, tools or a phone used for business.
  • Travel for work: business mileage, train fares and parking that relate to the work, kept separate from your personal travel.
  • Working from home: a reasonable share of household running costs where you work from home, within the rules HMRC sets.
  • Professional costs: accountancy, relevant insurance, and professional or trade memberships connected to your work.
  • Marketing: a website, advertising and the cost of finding new clients.
  • Stock and materials: the goods or raw materials you buy to sell or to make what you sell.

What usually is not allowable

Some costs feel like business spending but are not allowable, or only partly so. Anything that is really personal, the everyday clothes you would wear anyway, your normal lunches, the personal share of a mixed use phone, generally does not count in full. The mixed use point matters: where something is used for both business and personal life, you can usually only claim the business portion. Honest apportionment is the safe approach.

Why records are the whole game

You cannot claim what you cannot show. The difference between a self employed person who claims their full, legitimate expenses and one who overpays is almost never knowledge, it is record keeping. Keep your receipts, log your business mileage, and note the costs as they happen. A messy shoebox in January is how genuine expenses get forgotten and tax gets overpaid.

  1. Capture each business cost as it happens, with the receipt.
  2. Keep business spending separate from personal where you can.
  3. For mixed use items, note the business share honestly.
  4. Total your expenses for the year before you estimate your tax.

How expenses change your tax estimate

Because your expenses reduce your taxable profit, they directly change how much income tax and National Insurance you owe. A good tax estimate starts from your profit, your income after allowable costs, not your total takings. The table below shows the shape of it.

FAQ

Can I claim my phone and broadband?

Where you use them for the business, you can usually claim the business share, not the whole personal bill. Keep it honest and note how you worked out the split. The detailed rules are on the HMRC website.

Can I claim for working from home?

Yes, a reasonable share of home running costs where you genuinely work from home, within the methods HMRC allows. Many sole traders use a simplified approach, so check the current options before you decide.

What records do I need to keep?

Keep receipts and records of your income and costs, and hold on to them for as long as HMRC requires. Good ongoing records are what let you claim with confidence and answer any query later.

How do expenses affect how much tax I pay?

They reduce your taxable profit, which reduces your income tax and National Insurance. That is why recording them properly matters. To see the effect on your numbers, read our plain guide to self employed tax and estimate it in TaxSmith.
