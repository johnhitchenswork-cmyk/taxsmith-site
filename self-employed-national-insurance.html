National Insurance for the self-employed

National Insurance is the tax that most surprises people when they go self-employed, because it works differently from the version taken off an employed payslip, and because the rules have recently changed. This guide explains the two classes that apply to the self-employed, Class 2 and Class 4, with the correct current position on each. It also clears up the single most misunderstood point: that Class 2 is no longer a mandatory charge for a profitable sole trader.

A plain UK explanation for sole traders and freelancers, with the current rates for 2025/26 and 2026/27. General guidance, not formal tax advice.

The two classes that apply to you

As a self-employed person, two classes of National Insurance can be relevant. Class 2 was historically a small flat weekly charge that built your entitlement to the State Pension and some benefits. Class 4 is a percentage charge on your profit, closer in feel to an extra layer of Income Tax. Understanding which one costs you money today, and which one no longer does, is the whole point of this page.

Class 2 today: treated as paid, at no cost

This is where most articles and even some tools are out of date. Since 6 April 2024, self-employed people with profits at or above the Small Profits Threshold have Class 2 treated as paid at no cost. gov.uk states it plainly: Class 2 contributions are treated as having been paid to protect your National Insurance record, which means you do not have to pay them. For a profitable sole trader, the mandatory Class 2 charge is therefore zero.

The Small Profits Threshold is 6,845 pounds for 2025/26 and 7,105 pounds for 2026/27. Above it, Class 2 is treated as paid. Below it, Class 2 becomes voluntary rather than treated as paid: you can choose to pay it, at 3.50 pounds a week in 2025/26 and 3.65 pounds a week in 2026/27, to keep your National Insurance record complete. This is the page's key differentiator, because many calculators still add a Class 2 charge that is no longer due.

The correction in one line: a profitable self-employed person does not pay a mandatory Class 2 charge. Any tool that still adds roughly 179 pounds a year for Class 2 is using an old rule.

Class 4: the charge that does apply

Class 4 National Insurance is the one that costs a profitable sole trader money, and it is charged on profit. For both 2025/26 and 2026/27 the rates are the same: 6 percent on profit between 12,570 pounds and 50,270 pounds, then 2 percent on any profit above 50,270 pounds. It sits on top of your Income Tax and is worked out on the same profit figure.

Why it matters for your State Pension

The reason Class 2 exists at all is that it is the contribution that counts towards the State Pension and certain benefits. Class 4, despite costing more, does not build that entitlement in the same way. Under the current rules, having Class 2 treated as paid above the Small Profits Threshold means a profitable sole trader gets the pension-qualifying benefit without paying for it. If your profit is below the threshold, that is precisely when paying voluntary Class 2 can be worth considering, so a low-profit year does not leave a gap in your record. Checking your National Insurance record on gov.uk shows whether you have any gaps worth filling.

FAQ

Do self-employed people still pay Class 2 NI?

Not as a mandatory charge. Since 6 April 2024 it is treated as paid at no cost for those with profits above the Small Profits Threshold, and is voluntary below it, at 3.50 pounds a week in 2025/26 and 3.65 pounds a week in 2026/27.

How much is Class 4 National Insurance?

6 percent on profit between 12,570 and 50,270 pounds, then 2 percent above 50,270 pounds, for both 2025/26 and 2026/27. It is charged on your profit.

What is the Small Profits Threshold?

The profit level at or above which Class 2 is treated as paid: 6,845 pounds for 2025/26 and 7,105 pounds for 2026/27. Below it, Class 2 is voluntary.

Should I pay voluntary Class 2?

If your profit is below the Small Profits Threshold, voluntary Class 2 can keep your National Insurance record complete for the State Pension and some benefits. Whether it is worth it depends on your record, so check it on gov.uk.
